My JavaScript Journey

When I first got into tech, I was building backend applications with Ruby on Rails. It was clean, structured, and made a lot of sense to me. But around 2016, I made a conscious pivot to front-end development. I've always loved HTML and CSS. The immediacy, the creativity, and the direct connection to how users experience the web really drew me in.

JavaScript, on the other hand, didn’t click with me right away. It wasn’t intuitive at first. The learning curve was steep, and for a while, I avoided going too deep. What changed everything was discovering data visualization. Being able to turn raw, abstract data into something visual and emotionally resonant, something non-technical people could understand and care about, gave me a reason to push through the hard parts. That purpose gave JavaScript new meaning.

From there, I leaned into the front-end ecosystem. I worked with Angular, React on Rails, and eventually React itself. React’s declarative nature and composability made it easier to structure complex applications. I even worked with Ember.js on a few projects. The more I built, the more comfortable I became. JavaScript became a language I didn’t just use. I started thinking in it.

I’ve been fortunate to work on some large-scale projects that sharpened my skills and tested my resolve. At CBS Interactive, I contributed to Sportsline.com, where real-time data and performance were critical. I also worked on ComicBook.com and PopCulture.com, where we had to deliver fast, content-heavy user experiences to massive audiences.

Two of my biggest wins as a JavaScript engineer came from tackling performance and accessibility issues directly.

  • I led a deep cleanup of a tangled Webpack configuration which resulted in a 67 percent performance improvement. That win didn't just make our app faster. It reduced build times, sped up deployments, and gave the team back hours of productivity every week.
  • I also stepped in to resolve long-standing accessibility issues that were exposing the company to legal risk. I helped bring us into compliance faster than projected, avoiding the cost and complexity of a two million dollar lawsuit. That work wasn't flashy, but it was necessary and it made the product better for everyone.

JavaScript isn’t just code to me anymore. It’s how I build accessible, performant, impactful experiences at scale. It’s how I solve problems that matter, not just to engineers, but to users and stakeholders who rely on digital experiences every day.

Beyond my day-to-day engineering work, I’m most proud of founding and leading Vets Who Code. For over a decade, I’ve taught veterans and military spouses from around the world, helping them transition into tech careers. Through hands-on instruction, mentorship, and a supportive community, I’ve helped hundreds of veterans and spouses gain the skills and confidence to thrive in software engineering. Seeing their success stories—people who once wore the uniform now building the future of technology—reminds me why I do this work.
